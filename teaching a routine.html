Teaching a Routine to Mastery

Don't just teach a routine once; ensure students master it through clear steps and consistent practice.

1. Explain the "What" and "Why"

What It Is

Clearly defining the routine and explaining its importance to the class.

What It Looks Like

You say, "Today we're going to learn our routine for lining up. It's important so we can get to lunch quickly and safely."

Why It Matters

Students are more likely to follow a routine when they understand its purpose and benefit.

2. Model & Practice

What It Is

You demonstrate the routine correctly and then have students practice it themselves.

What It Looks Like

You walk to the door, show them how to stand in line, and then have the first row of students try it.

Why It Matters

Modeling provides a clear visual example, and practicing allows for hands-on learning and immediate feedback.

üëç

3. Provide Specific Feedback

What It Is

Giving students positive praise for following the routine and gentle corrections when they get off track.

What It Sounds Like

"I love how quietly you got in line, Alex!" or "Sonia, remember to leave some space between you and your friend."

Why It Matters

This reinforces the desired behavior and helps students internalize the routine correctly.

4. Reinforce & Reteach

What It Is

Consistently using the routine throughout the day and the year, and re-teaching it if students start to slip.

What It Looks Like

You remind students of the steps before lining up every day and, if necessary, take a moment to practice it again after a fire drill or recess.

Why It Matters

Routines only work if they are consistent. Constant reinforcement makes them an automatic habit for your students.
